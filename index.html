Learning Design
Across Contexts

How does learning design appear as a role, practice, artefact, and field of study across six practitioners in two focus groups? A qualitative synthesis informed by AI-assisted analysis and postdigital, access, and social justice frameworks.

Audio narration: Introduction

Read transcript

Welcome to our Group 3 artefact for EDN4601, Introduction to Learning Design. Our central question is simple but surprisingly slippery: how does learning design appear as a role, a practice, an artefact, and a field of study across very different contexts? To explore that, we conducted two focus groups with six practitioners working across higher education, government education, freelance and publishing, design support, and disciplinary teaching. Across both groups, one pattern became clear: learning design is defined less by job title and more by what people actually do. In every context, it involves mediating between content, learner needs, institutional constraints, and available technologies. This artefact brings those six voices together, relates them to classroom discussions and course theory, and shows how context shapes what learning design becomes.

01 · Methodology

Research Question & Approach

Interview Design Framework

The focus group questions were deliberately structured around the four course lenses of learning design: role, practice, artefact, and field of study. The sequence moved from broad professional context to specific examples, allowing participants first to situate their work institutionally and then to reflect on design processes, outputs, theoretical influences, access conditions, and evaluation.

This progression aligned with the task objective and encouraged detailed, open-ended responses rather than short definitional answers.

  • Role: How is learning design positioned in your institution or professional responsibilities?
  • Practice: Can you talk us through what happens when you design or redesign a learning experience?
  • Artefact: What kinds of outputs, resources, or learning products emerge from your design work?
  • Field of study: What theories, standards, frameworks, or professional learning shape your design decisions?

Interview Engagement and Facilitation

The discussions were conducted in a respectful, conversational manner, with participants building on one another’s examples and using follow-up prompts to deepen reflection. Questions such as “Can you give an example?”, “How does that work in practice?”, and “Who has final approval?” helped move the conversation from description to elaboration.

That interaction mattered analytically. It surfaced differences in autonomy, learner proximity, quality assurance, accessibility, and institutional constraint that would have remained hidden in shorter or more closed questioning formats.

"How does learning design appear as a role, practice, artefact, and field of study across different educational contexts?"

Core Research Question — EDN4601 Group 3 Assessment, 2026

Conducting two separate focus groups created an unexpected analytical advantage. Group A was more self-facilitated, while Group B was more structured. Despite those differences, both groups independently converged on the same core insight: learning design is defined less by what you are called and more by what you do. That convergence across sectors, institutional types, and employment models strengthens the claim that our findings reflect genuine patterns rather than one group’s framing.

02 · Contexts

Six Practitioners, Six Worlds

Context is not background — it fundamentally shapes what learning design becomes. Before analysis, we must understand who our participants are and the institutional ecologies they inhabit.

Audio narration: Contexts

Read transcript

Our six participants work in very different institutional worlds. Bilqis works as a learning technologist, translating subject matter intentions into structured LMS experiences. Megan leads online programme development in a private animation school and is trying to convert a hands-on diploma into an online format. Lizeka works in the Eastern Cape Department of Education, where the digital divide is not background noise but a central design condition. Jack works as a learning experience designer supporting lecturers at university level. Mduduzi brings a graphic design and accessibility lens to instructional design teams. And Dr Nkosi designs learning within the highly regulated space of engineering education. Put together, these contexts show that learning design is never context free; it is shaped by infrastructure, policy, accreditation, and proximity to learners.

03 · Four Lenses

Learning Design Through Four Lenses

Christine Immenga's framework (EDN4601, Week 1 webinar) identifies learning design as simultaneously a field of study, a professional role, a process, and a product. Each lens illuminates different evidence from our data.

Audio narration: Four Lenses Overview

Read transcript

We used four lenses from the course to analyse the interviews: learning design as role, practice, artefact, and field of study. These lenses helped us move beyond a narrow definition of the learning designer as a fixed profession. Instead, they allowed us to ask four better questions: who is doing design work, what are they doing, what are they producing, and what theories, standards, or forms of scholarship shape that work? Across our data, these four lenses overlap rather than sit neatly apart. That overlap is one of the most important findings of the artefact.

Audio narration: Role

Read transcript

The role lens revealed that learning design is a distributed function rather than a single fixed profession. Bilqis occupies a specialist contracted role. Lizeka shares design responsibility with curriculum staff and subject specialists. Megan has effectively absorbed the role while also trying to build institutional structures around it. Jack works in a formal learning experience design role, while Mduduzi contributes through visual and accessibility design, and Dr Nkosi carries design responsibility within his teaching role because no dedicated designer exists in his department. Across both groups, the pattern is clear: learning design is less about a title and more about responsibility for shaping learning experiences under specific contextual conditions.

A Distributed, Not Singular, Role

Across all six participants, learning design as a role does not reside in one fixed position. It surfaces as a distributed function shaped by institutional context, employment model, and available expertise. Three patterns emerged: a specialised dedicated role, distributed responsibility across a team, and design embedded in teaching.

Crucially, even the "specialised" role carries no single meaning. Bilqis's autonomy varies entirely by contract — sometimes fully prescribed, sometimes fully independent. The AI analysis initially framed this simply as "distributed between institutions," missing the employment model dimension entirely.

Fawns’s (2022, p. 715) concept of entangled pedagogy is illuminating here because it resists the idea that any single stakeholder holds full agency over what learning becomes. In Bilqis’s case, autonomy is renegotiated with each new contract. In Jack’s case, final approval rests with the lecturer. In Dr Nkosi’s case, major changes require departmental, faculty, ECSA, and industry approval. Entangled pedagogy does not just name a philosophy; it describes the lived institutional reality of these participants.

Fawns (2022, p. 715): "Agency is negotiated between teachers, students and other stakeholders" — never fixed in one role

Audio narration: Practice

Read transcript

The practice lens showed the strongest convergence across all six voices. In different ways, every participant described learning design as mediation. Bilqis translates between subject experts and the LMS. Megan translates hands-on vocational teaching into online experience. Lizeka translates exam results and infrastructure realities into micro-lessons and offline-capable resources. Jack redesigns activities when lecturers identify learning problems. Mduduzi translates difficult concepts into accessible visual explanations. And Dr Nkosi aligns outcomes, teaching activities, and assessment inside engineering constraints. The medium changes, but the mediating function remains. That is where the practice lens connects strongly to postdigital and entangled views of pedagogy from the course.

Mediation Between Pedagogy, Technology & Learner

Learning design as practice is fundamentally about mediation — navigating between pedagogical intent, technological possibility, and learner reality. No participant describes a linear, sequential process. All describe continuous negotiation.

Bilqis uses XAPI analytics to adjust content release dates based on learner engagement patterns. Megan asks what "consolidation" means without a physical classroom. Lizeka produces offline workbooks when connectivity fails. Dr Nkosi links every design decision to constructive alignment between outcomes, activities, and assessments.

A postdigital perspective helps us see why the same tools produce different educational realities. Moodle, H5P, video, and analytics are not the unit of analysis on their own. At Cambridge, Bilqis uses platform analytics to shape release decisions; in the Eastern Cape, Lizeka designs offline-capable workbooks because reliable access cannot be assumed. The postdigital lens shows that technology is always entangled with social, material, and infrastructural conditions.

Fawns (2019): Postdigital perspective — digital tools are always embedded in social, material and contextual activity; technology and pedagogy mutually shape each other

Learning design practice is a continuous negotiation — not a linear sequence. Fawns (2022) calls this "entangled pedagogy": technology, pedagogy, purposes, values and context are always mutually shaping. No element exists without the others.

01

Collaboration is Fundamental

Design is never solo across any context. Bilqis with SMEs and QA teams. Lizeka with subject advisors. Megan bridging academic and online delivery. Jack with lecturers. Mduduzi with instructional designers. Dr Nkosi with curriculum teams. All relational, always.

↳ Fawns (2022, p. 715): "Entangled pedagogy is collective; agency is negotiated across stakeholders" — never resident in one person alone

02

Technology Mediates — Context Shapes Use

All participants use an LMS, video, interactive tools — but how these function differs entirely. Moodle in the Eastern Cape is an offline-first accessibility challenge. Moodle at Cambridge is a sophisticated analytics environment. Same tool; different ecologies.

↳ Fawns (2019): Postdigital lens — tools are always embedded in social, material and contextual activity; never neutral

03

Learner Experience is Central

Every participant, regardless of title, foregrounds the learner. This is the shift Christine Immenga identified: from instructional design (teacher-centred) to learning design (learner-centred). Bilqis adjusts release dates by learner timezone. Mduduzi designs for accessibility. Lizeka advocates for learners with disabilities.

↳ Immenga (EDN4601, Week 1): "Learning design takes away the focus from the teacher and puts it on the student"

04

Institutional Constraints Shape Everything

Budget, policy, QA processes, employment model, platform decisions, accreditation requirements — all constitute design practice. Yet within constraints, all six participants exercise creative professional judgement. The constraint-agency tension is not incidental; it is constitutive of the role.

09 · Implications

What This Tells Us About Learning Design

Four implications grounded in evidence from Group A and Group B, connected to the course's theoretical foundations.

Audio narration: Implications

Read transcript

The implications of our findings are practical as well as conceptual. First, learning design capacity cannot be built through job titles alone, because design work is distributed across many roles. Second, context is constitutive of design, not peripheral to it. The digital divide, accreditation, contract conditions, and institutional strategy do not sit outside design decisions; they shape them from within. Third, accessible and equitable design is not a side issue. It appears across both groups, but at different stages of maturity, from emerging awareness to embedded standard to active research agenda. These findings suggest that learning design in African and Global South contexts must be understood through flexibility, responsiveness, and critical attention to conditions of access.

01

Learning design cannot be separated from context

The same tools, frameworks and titles mean different things across institutions. Designing well requires ecological understanding of context, not just technical skill. Fawns's (2022) entangled pedagogy insists that context is always already inside the design — not external to it.

02

The Global South demands its own design logic

Mainstream LD models embed connectivity assumptions that fail in Lizeka's Eastern Cape context. Roberts and Hernandez’s (2019, section 2.2) 5A’s framework — availability, affordability, awareness, ability, agency — provides tools for non-binary, context-sensitive design. Structural change requires institutional will, not just design creativity.

03

The field must professionalise — inclusively

Megan's enrollment in EDN4601 illustrates the demand for LD expertise and its institutional absence. The BOLD programme's micro-credential, stackable structure and RPL pathways (Gachago et al., 2025) represent a socially just model for growing the field across diverse entry points — including practitioners like Mduduzi who contribute without the title.

Gachago et al. (2025) draw on Veletsianos and Houlden’s (2020) idea of radical flexibility: equity requires more than flexible timing or format. It requires rethinking who gets recognised, what counts as expertise, and how access is structured. In this light, BOLD’s stackable pathways and recognition mechanisms move beyond affirmative fixes toward more transformative inclusion.

04

Autonomy and collaboration are in productive tension

All six participants navigate between constraint and creative agency. Bilqis prefers full autonomy but delivers under prescription. Megan needs quality control but values designer expertise. Dr Nkosi must seek approval for major changes. This tension is constitutive of the role — negotiated with professional judgement, not resolved by policy.

Three Key Corrections — Expanded

AI framed Bilqis's role as "distributed between institutions" — missing the employment model and its direct effect on autonomy, which she raised explicitly in the transcript.

AI Original

Learning design surfaces as a distributed role taken up differently by different people depending on context... positioning her work between pedagogy and technology.

Human Correction

Learning design is distributed not only between roles but also across employment models (contract vs. permanent) and its direct relation to designer autonomy. A contractor may be fully prescribed in one project and given complete creative authority in the next.

The AI implied Dr Nkosi was "filling a missing role" where no specialist is available. The group corrected this — his context is shaped by disciplinary accreditation requirements, not simply an absence.

AI Original

Dr Nkosi shows that in some departments, especially disciplinary ones, lecturers take on design responsibilities because there is no specialist designer available.

Human Correction

Dr Nkosi's context reflects strict professional body accreditation requirements that shape the design of learning. A general learning designer may struggle without background in the discipline — this is structural, not merely a staffing gap.

AI reduced Jack's self-description to "a support service for academic staff." The group identified that support service is only one of four frames he uses, and that flattening to this one misrepresents his positioning.

AI Original

Jack describes himself as a learning experience designer and he explicitly says that learning design is positioned as a support service for academic staff.

Human Correction

Jack's role occupies multiple frames simultaneously — as collaborative partner, field-building practitioner, and support function. "Support service" is only one of the frames he uses, and reducing his self-description to this single frame misrepresents the complexity of his positioning.

12 · Synthesis Conclusion

Drawing It Together: What Six Voices Tell Us About Learning Design

A final cross-group synthesis that draws the four lenses, seven themes, and comparative analysis into one argument.

Audio narration: Final Synthesis Conclusion

Read transcript

Drawing the six interviews together, our strongest conclusion is that learning design surfaces less as a fixed profession and more as a context-sensitive function. Across both focus groups, participants consistently described design work as mediation: between content and learner, pedagogy and platform, theory and implementation, aspiration and constraint. The differences matter too. Some participants work close to learners and analytics, others through intermediaries. Some have wide design agency, others work inside strong professional or institutional constraints. But those differences do not weaken the analysis. They strengthen it. They show that learning design changes shape across contexts while retaining a shared concern with educational coherence, learner support, and meaningful experience. That, for us, is what the six voices together reveal about learning design.

A spectrum of how learning design appears

The six participants occupy different positions between formal or specialised learning design roles and more embedded or distributed design work. The point of the spectrum is not to rank them, but to show how the same function appears under different institutional conditions.

The spectrum is interpretive rather than evaluative. The SVG badges signal the type of role emphasis each participant brings to learning design, helping the reader distinguish specialist, strategic, shared, visual, and embedded forms of design work at a glance.

Across both focus groups, a single argument emerges from the data: learning design is not just a role, or a process, or a product, or a field of study. It is all four simultaneously, distributed across people, institutions, and contexts that share no common infrastructure but are united by a common orientation — the deliberate effort to make learning more intentional, more accessible, and more aligned with the realities of the people doing the learning.

The six participants occupy positions along multiple spectrums: from specialist to embedded, from high autonomy to low, from analytics-driven to offline-first, and from reactive accommodation to field-building scholarship. Yet across every position, what Fawns (2022, p. 715) calls the entangled nature of educational design is visible. No participant controls the full design ecology. Every participant negotiates agency within constraints — contractual, structural, infrastructural, and disciplinary.

Perhaps the most important implication for learning design as a field is what Gachago et al. (2025) and the BOLD programme represent in this context: growing capacity by recognising the many people who already do design work in many forms and under many names. Mduduzi has no learning design title. Dr Nkosi has no learning designer in his department. Lizeka has no guaranteed connectivity. All three are still designing learning — and all three belong in the story of the field.
